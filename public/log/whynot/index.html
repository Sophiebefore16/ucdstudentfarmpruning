Why or Why Not ...?

Last updated: 2026/03/19

Before we start some serious rant about physiology and ecosystem in fruit tree management nowadays, let’s talk about dos and don’ts in pruning in another way. Here are some of the thoughts I have or come up with for someone who doesn’t really know about pruning while learning about fruit trees and pruning. I hope you will find the content at least not boring…!

Why not just leave them alone without pruning? How can you tell whether trees have feelings or not and they don’t hurt?

If we just don’t prune… Yeah you are right, we can actually do that but not for all trees. Evergreen trees like olives and citrus require little to none pruning, mainly for two reasons (that I can think of). First is that evergreen trees, as the name suggests, do not have significant dormant seasons compared to deciduous trees. In colder weather their growth does slow down in most cases but pruning still introduces stress to ongoing growth.

Second is about the leaf development. Leaves growing in the lower or more inward canopy receive less light while developing their leaf structure.

Leaf Structure, from *The Science of Plants*

A little high school bio, photosynthesis mainly goes on in these mesophyll cells which contain chloroplasts. Light penetrates palisade mesophyll to spongy mesophyll. In environments with lower light density (relatively darker), leaves would develop denser spongy mesophyll while palisade mesophyll would reduce in size and height. It is not the other way around as palisade mesophyll are designed to receive direct sunlight, and spongy mesophyll – as the name suggests, have more room for refraction and reflection, and maximize the amount of light captured by chloroplasts. This is also one of the reasons why we would want to prune in dormancy for deciduous trees since they have not developed leaves yet.

There are also other reasons. For some trees they are sensitive to stress and disease, so we don’t prune them. Farmers would like a denser tree for bearing more fruit (like almonds) or simply do not have enough time, people or money to prune 200 acres of trees. For trees we have on Student Farm, they definitely need some pruning so get your clippers and loppers ready in winter 😉.

Why pruning in the dormant season then? Is winter a good time for all pruning?

Imagine pruning in spring and summer with all the leaves, fruits, and flowers weight – that’s one reason. More importantly is that fungi, pests and diseases are usually dormant too. The cuttings are wounds and all the pathogen could get in.

HOWEVER, some stone fruit like apricot and cherry, who are the famous delicate princess duo, would prefer to be pruned in early spring or even summer after harvest. Since they are sensitive to any pathogen, pruning in winter when they are dormant slows down their defense mechanism to infection or seal the wound. In somewhere with a Mediterranean climate like Davis, fungi spores can also spread with the splash of rainfall, making winter pruning less ideal.

Why are you so interested in pruning?

I don’t know. It feels comforting and you genuinely reach a flow state while considering how each part interacts and also grows as a whole organism. Good way of practising systematic thinking. And it is also something I have some knowledge so I am more confident yapping about.
