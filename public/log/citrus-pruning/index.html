The Habsburgs of the Orchard

Last updated: 2026/05/18

In order to keep up with my presentation, I am writing about citrus pruning here. Yay! (actually I am burnt out)

When we are talking about citrus, what are we talking about?

I still remember people giggling when they heard “inbreed” in an intro bio class. Everyone will roll their eyes seeing The Habsburg family tree if you know the citrus family. All the citrus we know now mainly come from three ancestors.

The genetic lineage and hybridization of modern citrus fruits. Image credit: Velasco & Licciardello, 2014, via Nature Biotechnology. All rights reserved.

I am not an evolutionary biologist, so let’s just stop there. The point of this family tree is to show how close each “species” (some of them are not an independent species if the standard is different species’ interbreeding would not produce viable/fertile offspring) are. This is why when we discuss citrus, we tend to discuss them together, since most of the traits are super similar, unlike how diverse Rosaceae family fruit trees could be.

Does homogeneity make everything easier? Unfortunately, no. Citrus is still hard to prune, and the main reason is because it is evergreen. Almost every practice we have on citrus pruning is due to this feature. Why do we make such a fuss about pruning citrus but not really pruning like what we did to other fruit trees? You may have heard me saying citrus is just so delicate. But How?

The main reason is because we are greedy. We greedy humans want citrus everywhere; however, citrus does not want to be everywhere. Most of the cultivars we have are not fully adapted to temperate climate conditions like what we have in Davis. They are evergreen, which is an obvious hint of their origin - semi-tropical, with no frosting days and consistent sunlight. Davis does have intensive sunlight, but speaking of consistency, it does not do so well in winters. Another feature citrus hates is the daily temperature difference. It would do great on other fruits and vegetables, but citrus hate it. Usually in one of my hometowns where Pomelo is a major crop, the daily temperature difference is 8℃ or lower (the temperature difference cannot be convert to Fahrenheit since it is so stupid it does not even have a constant change, for your information it is usually 72℉ to 86℉), while in Davis it is at least 15℃ apart.

After complaining about the weather (I like it), we finally get to our main topic, how to prune citrus.

Timing

Little Sophie (not me) carries her pruning tools into the Student Farm and prunes everything she sees, except citrus. Oh no, how careless you can be, you might think, or a deeper question, why don’t we prune citrus in winter?

We don’t prune citrus in winter since new shoots or citrus are sensitive to potential frost. That doesn’t mean we cannot perform pruning at all, maintenance pruning, such as removing dead, diseased and crossing branches can be done on small branches, as long as it would not stimulate new growth. The best time to prune is after potential frost days and before summer heat.

Technique

Regular pruning tools also apply to citrus but do keep in mind that they have thin barks. Infection of your tools is critical in between trees to prevent cross infections. Sometimes even your clothes can contaminate new cuttings so try not to prune in a wet or foggy day and don’t rub your cloth against those trees (I know it sounds silly but people would unconsciously doing something like that I promise).

Decision

If you have read my first article, you now should know why we don’t want to prune evergreen trees that much and why every single decision is so critical – you cannot stress the tree while achieving similar goals pruning other trees.

What are some of the branches we have to prune? The first one is the gourmet; I usually call it water sprout. The bark is distinctively different on citrus trees, usually with lighter color. The branch is also thicker and tends to grow inwards. These branches would fruit but even if they fruit, the quality is worse and it consumes more nutrition.

Moving lower branches is also important to prevent contamination from pests and pathogens on the ground, which is called skirting. Mulching and fertilization would be hard with branches touching the ground. It could also contaminate the fruit.

Citrus canopy is not as obvious as other trees, but still, a too dense canopy blocks air flow and pests love that. Canopy management doesn’t need to be as intense as deciduous trees, but it is still essential in promoting fruiting woods. If you can see dappled sunlight on the ground, it should be okay. Other pruning may include limiting heights, which is pretty self-explanatory, I would not talk too much about.
